Offshoring and Technological Lock-In

single-authored

I study how offshoring and access to lower production costs affect firms’ innovation choices and whether this shifts them away from more novel technologies.

Show abstract

The ability to move parts of the production process offshore allows firms to reduce their unit costs through lower wage bills. I investigate how this change in their cost structure affects the exploration of new technologies. Previous studies have focused on the effect of offshoring on the volume of innovation and the trade-off between product and process innovation, whilst I focus on its effect on the technological direction of innovation. Using the economic integration of Central and Eastern European countries since the 1990s, I find that industries with a higher initial exposure to this offshoring shock are less likely to file patents in technology classes that are new to the sector. Patenting in these industries is also more concentrated in a narrower set of technology classes. These technologies tend to be legacy technologies that were already used before the offshoring shock. I rationalise these patterns in a simple two-period model in which a firm chooses between continuing with a legacy technology or replacing it with a superior new version. Offshoring increases the opportunity cost of switching technologies, delaying technological transition.
